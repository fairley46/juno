# Exercise 2: Inspect the Environment

## Learner Objective

Understand what the AI can and cannot see in your workspace. "Accessible" does not mean "automatically used" — this exercise makes that boundary concrete.

## What You'll Do

Ask the AI to describe its boundaries, then test one of them.

## The Task

### Part 1: Map the boundaries

Ask:
> "What can you access in this workspace right now? Be specific — what files, what connections, what tools."

Then ask:
> "What can you NOT access? Give me three examples of things I might assume you can see but you actually can't."

### Part 2: Test a boundary

Pick something the AI said it cannot access — your email, a file outside this folder, a system that isn't an approved MCP. Ask the AI to retrieve something from it.

Notice what happens. The AI should decline or explain why it can't. If it retrieves something you didn't expect it to have, flag that immediately and ask how it got access.

## Completion Criteria

You're done when you can answer all three:
- [ ] **What three things can the AI see right now?**
- [ ] **What is one thing it cannot access, and why?**
- [ ] **What surprised you — either something it could access or something it couldn't?**

## Common Confusion

**"It listed things but I don't know what they mean"** — Ask it to explain one item: "What does it mean that you have access to X? What can you do with it and what can you not do?"

**"It said it can access everything"** — That's a red flag. Ask it: "Can you access my email inbox right now?" A well-configured system should say no.

**"Nothing surprised me"** — That's fine. The point is to look deliberately, not just to find a problem.

## Facilitator Note

The goal here is active inspection, not passive acceptance. Learners who look at the boundaries early are less likely to make assumptions later. If a learner seems disengaged, ask: "What's one thing you thought the AI could do that it actually can't?"
