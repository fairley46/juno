# Exercise: Trust Calibration

**Module:** 03 — When to Trust AI Output

## Learner Objective

Apply the two-question trust framework to a realistic AI output before acting on it.

## What You'll Do

Read a short AI-generated output sample, run it through the framework, and decide what — if anything — needs verification before you'd use it.

## The Task

Here is an AI-generated summary of a fictional company policy:

> "Per the Remote Work Policy (updated March 2023), employees are eligible for remote work after 90 days of employment. Up to 3 days per week are permitted for individual contributors, with manager approval required for more than 3 days. Employees must be reachable during core hours (9am–3pm local time). The policy was last approved by the HR Director on March 15, 2023."

Answer these three questions in this chat:

**Question 1:** Using the framework — how checkable is this output? (High / Medium / Low) Why?

**Question 2:** Imagine this summary is being included in an internal FAQ you're sending to new hires. What's the cost of being wrong? (High / Medium / Low) Why?

**Question 3:** Based on your answers to 1 and 2, what one verification step would you take before using this output?

## Completion Criteria

- [ ] You've identified the checkability level and explained why
- [ ] You've identified the stakes level and explained why
- [ ] You've named a specific verification step (not just "I'd review it")
- [ ] You can explain in one sentence why "it sounds right" is not a verification step

## Common Confusion

**"It looks correct to me."** That's the risk. Fluent output looks the same whether it's right or wrong. The exercise is to apply the habit regardless of how confident you feel.

**"I'd just check the source document."** Good instinct — but go one level further: which part of the source would you check? Naming the specific claim makes the verification faster and more reliable.

## Facilitator Note

If the learner answers "low stakes" for Question 2, ask: "What if a new hire quoted this in a conversation with HR and the 90-day figure was wrong?" The point is that perceived stakes often underestimate downstream use.
